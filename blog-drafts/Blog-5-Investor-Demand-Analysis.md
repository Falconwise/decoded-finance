## Oversubscription Basics: The Math Behind the Headlines

Let's start with precise definitions and calculations.

### What Is Oversubscription?

**Definition:** The ratio of total investor demand to shares available for sale.

**Formula:**
```
Oversubscription = Total Demand ÷ Shares Available
```

**Example: Simple Case**
- Shares available: 10 million
- Investor orders: 100 million shares
- **Oversubscription: 10x** (or 1,000%)

### Institutional vs Retail Oversubscription

Saudi IPOs measure these separately because allocation rules differ:

**Institutional Tranche (Typically 70% of offering):**
```
Institutional Oversubscription = Institutional Orders ÷ Institutional Allocation

Example:
- Institutional allocation: 7 million shares
- Institutional orders: 700 million shares
- Institutional oversubscription: 100x
```

**Retail Tranche (Historically 30%, now CMA guidance):**
```
Retail Oversubscription = Retail Subscription Value ÷ Retail Allocation Value

Example:
- Retail allocation: 3 million shares × SAR 50/share = SAR 150M available
- Retail subscriptions: SAR 600M received
- Retail oversubscription: 4x (or 400%)
```

### Why Separate Metrics Matter

**Institutional oversubscription** signals:
- Professional investor appetite (smart money validation)
- Aftermarket support (institutions hold longer-term)
- Pricing power (can command premium if 50x+)

**Retail oversubscription** signals:
- Brand awareness and consumer appeal
- Aftermarket volatility potential (retail = shorter holding periods)
- Retail allocation adequacy (underallocated retail = frustrated investors)

## The Oversubscription Paradox: Higher Isn't Always Better

Here's a counterintuitive insight: **Extreme oversubscription (>200x) can signal underpricing, not success.**

### The "Money Left on the Table" Problem

**Case: Alwazn Almithaly - 450x Oversubscription**

**What Happened:**
- Offered shares at price X
- Received 450x orders
- **Implication:** Could have priced 2-3x higher and still been fully subscribed

**The Math:**
- Actual pricing: SAR 50/share (hypothetical)
- Institutional demand: 450x
- **Could have priced at:** SAR 100-120/share (2-2.4x higher) and still achieved 150-200x oversubscription

**Value Left Behind:**
```
Offer size at SAR 50: SAR 346M
Offer size at SAR 100: SAR 692M (same shares, double price)
"Lost" capital: SAR 346M
```

### When High Oversubscription Is Good vs Bad

**Good (Efficient Pricing):**
- **10-30x institutional:** Strong demand, left modest upside for first-day pop
- **200-400% retail:** Enthusiastic retail participation, good brand signal
- **Pricing at top of range:** Captured maximum approved valuation

**Bad (Underpricing):**
- **>200x institutional:** Significantly mispriced, left huge value on table
- **First-day returns >20%:** Confirms underpricing (could have raised more)
- **Pricing at bottom of range:** Conservative, left money behind

**Optimal Target:**
- **30-80x institutional:** Robust demand without extreme underpricing
- **300-600% retail:** Strong enthusiasm, manageable allocation
- **First-day return 5-10%:** Efficient pricing, modest pop for investors

## The CMA's New 30% Retail Allocation Guidance (October 2025)

A significant policy shift is reshaping Saudi IPO dynamics.

### What Changed

**Previous Practice (Pre-October 2025):**
- Institutional investors dominated: **70-80% allocations**
- Retail treated as "bonus" tranche: 20-30% allocations
- Underwriters prioritized institutional relationships

**New CMA Guidance (October 2025):**
- Issuers **encouraged to allocate ~30% to retail investors**
- Explicit policy statement from CMA
- Aligns with Saudi Vision 2030 wealth distribution goals

### Why the CMA Made This Change

**1. Wealth Distribution Agenda:**
- Government privatizations target **citizen wealth creation**
- Broader stock ownership = national economic participation
- Reduce concentration in institutional hands

**2. Liquidity Enhancement:**
- **Broader shareholder base** improves aftermarket trading
- Retail investors provide buy-side support (averaging down)
- Reduces single-institution dominance (price stability)

**3. Brand Building for Companies:**
- **Consumer-facing companies** benefit from customer-shareholders
- Retail investors = product/service advocates (free marketing)
- Creates "community ownership" feeling

**4. Market Development:**
- **6.5 million retail brokerage accounts** in Saudi Arabia
- Activating retail participation = market deepening
- Reduces reliance on institutional capital (diversification)

### Impact on IPO Strategy

**Companies Must Now:**

**Build Retail Appeal (Not Just Institutional Story):**
- Emphasize brand recognition in prospectus
- Highlight Vision 2030 alignment (retail investors respond to national themes)
- Simplify equity story (retail = less sophisticated than institutions)

**Marketing Shift:**
- Traditional roadshow: 80% institutional meetings
- New approach: **40% institutional + 30% media/retail engagement + 30% digital marketing**
- Retail advertising campaigns (financial press, social media)

**Pricing Considerations:**
- Retail investors less price-sensitive than institutions (brand loyalty)
- Can command **5-10% premium** if strong retail appeal
- But retail underallocation (if oversubscribed) = post-IPO selling pressure

## What Drives Institutional Demand? The Professional Investor Mindset

Understanding what institutions look for helps maximize their oversubscription.

### Key Institutional Demand Drivers

**1. Valuation vs Peers (Multiple Arbitrage)**

Institutions run detailed comparables analysis:
```
Your IPO P/E: 14x
Peer 1 P/E: 18x
Peer 2 P/E: 16x
Peer 3 P/E: 17x

Average peer: 17x
Your discount: 18% below peers

Institutional logic: "Buy at 14x, it re-rates to 16-17x = 15-20% return"
```

**High demand if:** Your IPO trades at **10-20% discount** to established peers (arbitrage opportunity)

**Low demand if:** Your IPO priced **at or above** peer average (no upside, more risk)

---

**2. Growth Trajectory vs Market**

Institutions model revenue and EBITDA growth:
```
Your company CAGR: 22% (next 3 years)
Sector average CAGR: 12%

Premium justified: 22% ÷ 12% = 1.83x
Your P/E: 16x
Peer average P/E: 14x
Your premium: 14% (1.14x)

Institutional logic: "Growing 1.83x faster but only 1.14x more expensive = undervalued"
```

**High demand if:** **Growth premium < Growth multiple** (paying less than growth justifies)

**Low demand if:** Growth premium = or > Growth multiple (fully valued or overpriced)

---

**3. Free Float and Liquidity**

Institutions need exit confidence:
```
Small cap with 20% float: Hard to exit SAR 100M position (price impact)
Mid cap with 30% float: Can exit SAR 100M over 2-3 weeks
Large cap with 35% float: Can exit SAR 100M in 3-5 days
```

**High demand if:** **Free float 30%+** (liquid exit assured)

**Low demand if:** Free float <25% (liquidity concerns, demand discount)

---

**4. Post-IPO Lock-Up Structure**

Institutions analyze founder/insider lock-ups:
```
Scenario A: Founders locked up 12 months post-IPO
- Supply overhang in Year 2 (institutions discount for this)

Scenario B: Founders locked up 24 months, gradual release
- Lower supply risk = higher institutional bids
```

**High demand if:** **24+ month lock-ups with staggered release** (supply predictability)

**Low demand if:** Short lock-ups (6-12 months) or cliff release (supply shock risk)

---

**5. Sector Momentum and Thematic Alignment**

Institutions allocate based on sector views:

**2025 Saudi Hot Sectors (High Institutional Demand):**
- **Healthcare:** Vision 2030 medical tourism, aging demographics
- **Logistics:** E-commerce growth, infrastructure expansion
- **Aviation/Tourism:** Connectivity boom, Neom, Red Sea Project
- **Consumer Discretionary:** Rising incomes, spending power
- **Technology:** Digital transformation, fintech adoption

**Cold Sectors (Lower Institutional Demand):**
- Traditional retail (e-commerce disruption concerns)
- Industrial (cyclical, capital-intensive, low margins)
- Real estate (regulatory uncertainty, market oversupply)

## What Drives Retail Demand? The Individual Investor Mindset

Retail investors have different psychology and constraints.

### Key Retail Demand Drivers

**1. Brand Recognition and Familiarity**

Retail investors buy what they know:
```
Example: Flynas IPO
- Every Saudi has flown Flynas or seen purple planes
- Brand = trust = willingness to invest
- "I fly Flynas, so I'll invest in Flynas"
```

**High retail demand if:** **National brand, consumer touchpoint, daily interaction**

**Low retail demand if:** B2B company, industrial, low consumer visibility

---

**2. Story Simplicity**

Retail needs simple equity story:
```
Good: "We deliver food to your home. Delivery market growing 30% annually."
Bad: "We provide integrated logistics solutions leveraging synergistic supply chain optimization."
```

**High retail demand if:** **One-sentence business description, clear growth driver**

**Low retail demand if:** Complex business model, multiple segments, hard to understand

---

**3. Sharia Compliance Status**

**Critical for Saudi retail:**
- **78% of Saudi retail investors prefer Sharia-compliant equities**
- Sharia-compliant IPOs attract **3-5x more retail subscriptions**
- Non-compliant = 60-70% of retail investor pool excluded

**Sharia Compliance Criteria:**
- Revenue from permissible activities (no alcohol, gambling, conventional interest)
- Debt-to-market-cap <33%
- Cash + interest-bearing securities <33% of assets
- Accounts receivable <45% of assets

**High retail demand if:** **Sharia-compliant certification from reputable scholars**

**Low retail demand if:** Non-compliant or uncertain status

---

**4. Minimum Investment Threshold**

Retail investors face capital constraints:
```
Scenario A: SAR 50/share, 10-share minimum = SAR 500 minimum investment
- Accessible to most retail (median subscription SAR 5,000-10,000)

Scenario B: SAR 200/share, 10-share minimum = SAR 2,000 minimum
- Excludes smaller retail investors
```

**High retail demand if:** **Minimum investment SAR 500-1,000** (accessibility)

**Low retail demand if:** High share price = high minimum (excludes mass market)

---

**5. Expected First-Day Pop**

Retail investors seek quick gains:
```
If prospectus priced conservatively:
- Retail expects 10-20% first-day gain
- "Flip" strategy = subscribe, sell on day 1

If prospectus priced aggressively:
- Retail fears flat/negative day 1
- Reduces subscription enthusiasm
```

**High retail demand if:** **Pricing perceived conservative** (room for pop)

**Low retail demand if:** Pricing perceived full/aggressive (limited upside)

## The Allocation Challenge: Managing Oversubscription

High oversubscription creates difficult allocation decisions.

### Institutional Allocation Mechanics

**Pro-Rata with Floors and Ceilings:**

Example: 100x oversubscription, 70% institutional tranche
```
Institutional tranche: 7M shares available
Orders received: 700M shares (100x)
Allocation factor: 1% (1 ÷ 100)

Institution A ordered: 50M shares → Receives 500K shares (1%)
Institution B ordered: 20M shares → Receives 200K shares (1%)
```

**But with adjustments:**
- **Anchor investors:** Pre-committed 30-40% (guaranteed allocation)
- **Strategic investors:** Long-term holders get premium allocation
- **Flippers:** Known short-term traders get minimal allocation

**Underwriter Discretion:**
- Allocation not purely mathematical
- Relationship-driven (reward loyal institutional clients)
- Strategic considerations (prefer quality long-term holders)

---

### Retail Allocation Mechanics

**Simple Pro-Rata (No Discretion):**

Example: 4x retail oversubscription, 30% retail tranche
```
Retail tranche value: SAR 150M available
Subscriptions received: SAR 600M (4x)
Allocation factor: 25% (1 ÷ 4)

Retail investor A subscribed: SAR 10,000 → Receives SAR 2,500 allocation
Retail investor B subscribed: SAR 50,000 → Receives SAR 12,500 allocation
```

**No Preference System:**
- All retail treated equally (small and large)
- Pure mathematical pro-rata
- Refunds processed within 2-3 days

---

### The Underallocation Problem

**High oversubscription = Frustrated investors:**

Example: Flynas 350% retail oversubscription
```
Retail investor subscribes: SAR 20,000
Receives allocation: SAR 5,700 (28.5%)
Refund: SAR 14,300

Outcome: Investor wanted more exposure but couldn't get it
Risk: May sell allocation immediately to "lock in gains" (aftermarket pressure)
```

**Managing This Risk:**
- Price IPO to allow 10-15% first-day gain (satisfies frustrated retail)
- Strong institutional base (supports price if retail sells)
- Gradual release strategy (not all retail sells day 1)

## Key Takeaways: Mastering Investor Demand

1. **Oversubscription ≠ Success:** 450x oversubscription can mean severe underpricing (money left on table); optimal is 30-80x institutional, 300-600% retail

2. **2025 Demand Leaders:** Saudi Parts (241x), Fakeeh Care (162x), Flynas (100x institutional, 350% retail) driven by brand recognition, defensive sectors, Vision 2030 alignment

3. **Nomu's Small Deal Effect:** Khaldi Logistics 1,949% oversubscription reflects mathematical artifact (SAR 75M denominator, not exceptional demand quality)

4. **CMA's 30% Retail Guidance:** October 2025 policy shift requires companies to build retail appeal, not just institutional story; impacts marketing, pricing, brand strategy

5. **Institutional Drivers:** Valuation discount to peers (15-20% optimal), growth premium <growth multiple, 30%+ free float, 24-month lock-ups, sector momentum

6. **Retail Drivers:** Brand recognition, story simplicity, Sharia compliance (78% of Saudi retail prefers), accessible minimum investment (SAR 500-1,000), expected first-day pop

7. **Sharia Compliance Impact:** 3-5x more retail subscriptions for compliant IPOs; critical for consumer-facing companies targeting broad retail participation

8. **Allocation Mathematics:** Institutional pro-rata with discretion (anchor preference), retail pure pro-rata (no preference); high oversubscription = frustrated investors

9. **Optimal Pricing Strategy:** Leave 5-10% upside for first-day pop (efficient pricing), avoid >20% pop (underpricing signal), price at top of range if demand 50x+

10. **Strategic Sequencing:** Build brand 3-6 months pre-IPO → Secure Sharia compliance → Price conservatively → Target 30-80x institutional demand → Allocate strategically
