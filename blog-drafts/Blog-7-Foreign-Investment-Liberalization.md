# The February 2026 Game-Changer: How Foreign Investor Liberalization Reshapes Saudi IPO Valuations

## Introduction

**February 1, 2026 marked the most significant structural change to Saudi Arabia's capital markets in a decade.**

On that date, the Capital Market Authority (CMA) eliminated the Qualified Foreign Investor (QFI) concept, granting **direct foreign investor access to TASI** (the main market) for the first time. No intermediation, no swap agreements, no complex licensing—just straightforward investment access for international institutions and individuals.

The immediate impact:
- **4.87% current foreign ownership** (SAR 466B of SAR 9.56T TASI market cap)
- **Projected 8-12% within 2 years** (SAR 350-600B additional inflows)
- **+5-8% TASI valuation boost** for internationally attractive sectors
- **TASI/Nomu premium widens by 3-5 percentage points** (Nomu remains domestically focused)

But the implications extend far beyond headline numbers. This policy shift fundamentally alters:
- **IPO venue selection calculus** (TASI's foreign access now decisive advantage)
- **Sector valuation hierarchies** (international themes get re-priced upward)
- **Post-IPO shareholder composition** (global institutional participation)
- **Competitive positioning** (TASI companies access deeper capital pools than Nomu)

In this article, we'll decode exactly what changed, quantify the valuation impact, identify which sectors benefit most, and provide strategic guidance for companies planning IPOs in this new landscape.

## What Changed: The QFI Elimination Explained

### The Old Regime (Pre-February 1, 2026)

**Foreign Access Mechanism:**
- Foreign investors required **Qualified Foreign Investor (QFI) license**
- Application process through Tadawul
- Minimum capital requirements
- Documentation and compliance burden
- **Indirect access via swap agreements** (many international funds used derivatives rather than direct ownership)

**Practical Effect:**
- Only **sophisticated foreign institutions** navigated QFI system
- Retail foreign investors essentially excluded
- **Complexity tax:** 2-3% discount embedded in TASI valuations due to access friction
- Foreign ownership capped at sector-specific limits (mostly removed by 2020, but QFI structure remained)

**Nomu Impact:**
- QFI rules applied to Nomu as well
- Plus Nomu's own "qualified investor" restrictions (domestic institutional-only pre-listing)
- **Result:** Nomu effectively closed to foreign capital (0.5-1.5% foreign ownership typical)

---

### The New Regime (Post-February 1, 2026)

**Direct Foreign Access:**
- **No QFI license required** for TASI investment
- Simplified account opening for non-residents
- Standard KYC/AML procedures (no Saudi-specific requirements)
- **Same access as Saudi nationals** (universal market access)

**Who Can Now Invest:**
- Foreign institutional investors (pension funds, sovereign wealth, mutual funds)
- Foreign retail investors (individuals from any country)
- Previously excluded categories now eligible

**Nomu Status:**
- **Remains unchanged**: Nomu still has qualified investor restrictions
- Foreign investors must meet Saudi qualified investor criteria (SAR 40M+ securities, or professional certification)
- **Practical impact:** Nomu stays 98%+ domestic ownership

---

### Why the CMA Made This Change

**1. Vision 2030 Capital Markets Development:**
- Goal: Make Saudi Arabia a global financial hub
- Benchmark: UAE's Abu Dhabi (MSCI Emerging Market status)
- Foreign ownership depth = market credibility signal

**2. MSCI Emerging Market Inclusion Push:**
- MSCI requires **accessible markets** for emerging status
- QFI complexity was barrier to upgrade from Frontier to Emerging
- Direct access removes objection (eligibility improved)

**3. Liquidity Enhancement:**
- Deeper investor base = tighter bid-ask spreads
- Foreign capital reduces domestic market volatility
- 24/7 global capital allocation (not just regional flows)

**4. Valuation Premium for Saudi Companies:**
- Global comparison: Emerging markets with foreign access trade at **8-12% premium** vs restricted markets
- Competitive with GCC peers (UAE, Qatar have open access)
- Attracts higher-quality IPOs (companies choose venues with best valuations)

### Nomu Foreign Ownership Reality

**Aggregate Numbers:**
- Total Nomu market cap: **SAR 40.5 billion** (January 2026)

**Why So Low?**

3. Lack of analyst coverage (international banks don't cover Nomu)

```
TASI foreign ownership: 4.87%

Future ratio: TASI will have 5-6x more foreign participation
```

**Critical Insight:** The February 2026 liberalization **widens the TASI/Nomu gap** because only TASI benefits—Nomu's structure remains domestically oriented.

## Valuation Impact: Quantifying the Foreign Access Premium

### The +5-8% TASI Valuation Boost

**Mechanism:** Direct foreign access reduces the "complexity discount" and adds demand layers.

**Components of Valuation Lift:**

**1. Elimination of Complexity Discount (2-3% gain):**
- Pre-Feb 2026: QFI friction embedded 2-3% discount in TASI multiples
- Post-Feb 2026: Complexity removed, discount disappears
- **Impact:** Baseline 2-3% re-rating purely from access simplification

**2. Incremental Demand (2-3% gain):**
- New foreign capital: SAR 350-600B over 2 years
- Current TASI cap: SAR 9.56T
- Incremental demand: 3.7-6.3% of market cap
- Assuming 50% absorption rate (rest goes to new liquidity): **2-3% price impact**

**3. Multiple Expansion from Comparability (1-2% gain):**
- International investors apply global sector multiples
- Saudi multiples often trade at discounts to global peers (access friction)
- Direct access = Saudi multiples converge toward global benchmarks
- **Impact:** 1-2% compression of Saudi-specific discount

**Total Expected Gain: 5-8%** (sum of three components)

  - Rising middle class narrative
  - International comparables (global retail multiples)

**Tier 2: Moderate Benefit (+4-6%)**
- **Banking & Financial Services:**
  - Already has 7.2% foreign ownership (mature foreign participation)
  - Dividend yield attractive but incremental demand moderate
  - Regulatory familiarity (global banks understand banking)

- **Petrochemicals:**
  - Commodity exposure already accessible via QFI (less new demand)
  - Global institutions already invested (6.8% current ownership)
  - Benefit from liquidity improvement more than access per se

  - Regulated utilities = stable but limited growth

**Tier 3: Minimal Benefit (+1-3%)**

  - Less international appeal (domestic focus)
  - Capital-intensive with longer payback periods
  - Limited comparables for foreign investors to benchmark

**Strategic Takeaway:** Companies in **aviation, tourism, healthcare, consumer** sectors should emphasize international investor access in IPO marketing—it's a genuine 7-10% valuation advantage post-Feb 2026.

## What Foreign Investors Want: The Due Diligence Checklist

Understanding foreign investor priorities helps optimize IPO positioning.

### Key Foreign Investor Requirements

**1. English Disclosure Quality:**
- Prospectus translated professionally (not machine translation)
- Quarterly reports in English (not Arabic-only)
- Management speaks English fluently (investor calls)
- **Impact:** Poor English disclosure = foreign investors pass

**2. International Accounting Standards:**
- IFRS preferred (vs local GAAP)
- Audited by Big 4 firm (not local-only auditor)
- Comparable to global peers (same metrics, presentation)
- **Impact:** IFRS adoption = 3-5% valuation premium from foreign investors

**3. Corporate Governance Transparency:**
- Independent directors (33%+ board, international experience preferred)
- Audit committee with external experts
- Related-party transaction disclosure (full transparency)
- ESG reporting framework (increasingly required)
- **Impact:** Strong governance = foreign institutional eligibility

**4. Liquidity Assurance:**
- Free float 30%+ (minimum for foreign institutions)
- Daily volume SAR 10M+ (exit ability crucial)
- Market makers committed (tight spreads)
- **Impact:** Low liquidity = foreign investors demand 10-15% discount

**5. Sector Understandability:**
- Business model matches global comparables (not Saudi-specific complexity)
- Growth drivers explained in international context (not just domestic)
- Competitive positioning vs regional/global peers (not just local)
- **Impact:** Complex local stories = foreign investors skip
