## First-Day Returns: The Underpricing Phenomenon

### What Is IPO Underpricing?

**Definition:** The percentage gain from offer price to first-day closing price.

**Formula:**
```
First-Day Return = (Day 1 Close Price - Offer Price) ÷ Offer Price × 100%
```

**Example:**
- Offer price: SAR 50
- First-day close: SAR 54.25
- First-day return: **+8.5%**

**Why Underpricing Occurs:**
1. **Asymmetric information:** Issuers know company better than investors
2. **Risk aversion:** Underwriters leave "money on the table" to ensure success
3. **Aftermarket support:** Positive first day creates momentum (self-fulfilling)
4. **Winner's curse:** Winning IPO allocation might signal overpricing (investors discount)

**Distribution:**
- 0-5% returns: 25% of IPOs
- 5-10% returns: 30% of IPOs
- 10-20% returns: 30% of IPOs
- >20% returns: 15% of IPOs

**Key Observation:** Nomu shows **higher average underpricing** (+12.3% vs +8.5%) and **wider variance** (SD 11.2% vs 7.3%), reflecting:
- Less efficient price discovery (smaller, less liquid market)
- Higher pricing uncertainty (growth-stage companies)
- Lower institutional sophistication (retail-dominated aftermarket)

### Case Studies: Extremes of First-Day Performance

**Massive First-Day Pop: 30%+ Returns**

**What Happened:**
- Company priced conservatively at bottom of range
- Unexpected positive news during subscription period
- Extreme oversubscription (200x+)
- Institutional FOMO (fear of missing out)

**Example Pattern:**
```
Offer price: SAR 40 (bottom of SAR 40-50 range)
Day 1 open: SAR 52 (+30%)
Day 1 close: SAR 54 (+35%)
```

**Interpretation:**
- **Severe underpricing:** Left SAR 350M+ on table (30% of offering)
- Could have priced at SAR 52 and still been fully subscribed
- Existing shareholders capture gain, but company got less capital

**Winner:** First-day investors (flippers)
**Loser:** Company and selling shareholders (foregone proceeds)

---

**Negative First Day: -2% to -5% Returns**

**What Happened:**
- Company priced aggressively at top of range
- Market conditions deteriorated post-pricing
- Institutional allocation too concentrated (limited aftermarket buyers)
- Weak retail demand

**Example Pattern:**
```
Offer price: SAR 60 (top of SAR 55-60 range)
Day 1 open: SAR 58 (-3.3%)
Day 1 close: SAR 57 (-5%)
```

**Interpretation:**
- **Overpricing:** Demand insufficient to support top-of-range valuation
- Retail investors underwater immediately (selling pressure)
- Institutional flippers exit (no first-day gain to capture)

**Winner:** None (bad signal for all)
**Loser:** Company reputation, investor sentiment, future capital access

---

### The "Goldilocks" First-Day Return: 5-10%

**Why This Range Is Optimal:**

**For Companies:**
- Maximized capital raised (priced near demand ceiling)
- Left modest upside for investors (goodwill)
- Strong aftermarket signal (demand > supply)
- Minimized "money left on table" (<10% underpricing acceptable)

**For Investors:**
- Modest gain validates IPO participation
- Sufficient return to hold (not immediate flip)
- Strong base for 6-month+ performance

**For Underwriters:**
- Demonstrates pricing skill (balanced supply/demand)
- Happy clients (company + investors)
- Reputation boost for future mandates

**Saudi 2025 Examples in Goldilocks Range:**
- Fakeeh Care: +6.5% first day (SAR 58 offer → SAR 61.77 close)
- Saudi Parts Store: +7.2% first day
- Al Baha Investment: +5.8% first day

**Common Pattern:** These deals priced at **top of range with 50-100x oversubscription**, indicating efficient price discovery.

## The 23-Point TASI/Nomu Performance Gap: Why?

**TASI 6-month:** +4.2%  
**Nomu 6-month:** -15%  
**Differential:** **19 percentage points**

### Root Causes of Performance Divergence

**1. Market Structure: Liquidity Flight During Stress**

2025 was a down market year—this **stress test** revealed structural differences:

**TASI Resilience Factors:**
- Daily volume SAR 12-45M per stock (institutional support)
- Tight bid-ask spreads (0.2-0.5%) = lower transaction costs
- Passive index inflows (FTSE, MSCI buying regardless of sentiment)
- Foreign investor participation (4.87% ownership = stabilizing force)

**Nomu Vulnerability Factors:**
- Daily volume SAR 0.8-3M per stock (forced selling amplified)
- Wide bid-ask spreads (1-3%) = exit difficult
- No passive inflows (excluded from major indices)
- Minimal foreign ownership (1.49% = purely domestic sentiment)

**Result:** Risk-off environment hit illiquid Nomu **3.8x harder** (-26% vs -6.8% index returns).

---

**2. Issuer Quality: Selection Standards**

**TASI Listing Requirements (Higher Bar):**
- Market cap minimum: SAR 300M
- 3+ years audited financials
- Consistent profitability required
- Stringent governance (33% independent directors)
- Free float 30% (liquidity depth)

**Nomu Listing Requirements (Lower Bar):**
- Market cap minimum: SAR 10M
- 2 years audited financials
- Profitability not mandatory
- Lighter governance (fewer independent directors)
- Free float 20% (lower liquidity)

**Outcome:** TASI's higher standards **filter for quality**—only 35% of TASI IPOs trade below offer at 6 months vs 58% for Nomu.

---

**3. Institutional Sponsorship**

**TASI IPOs:**
- 70% institutional allocation (long-term holders)
- Lock-up periods 12-24 months (supply control)
- Analyst coverage from launch (information flow)
- Active aftermarket support from underwriters

**Nomu IPOs:**
- 50-70% institutional (but smaller institutions)
- Shorter lock-ups (6-12 months typical)
- Limited analyst coverage (smaller market caps)
- Minimal aftermarket support (economically unviable for small stocks)

**Result:** TASI stocks have **stronger hands** holding shares, reducing volatility and supporting prices during downturns.

---

**4. Retail Investor Behavior**

**TASI Retail Characteristics:**
- 30% allocation (CMA guidance)
- Holding periods 3-6 months average
- "Buy and hold" mentality (consumer brands, defensive sectors)

**Nomu Retail Characteristics:**
- 30-50% allocation (smaller institutional base)
- Holding periods 1-3 months average
- "Flip for quick gain" mentality (growth-stage speculation)

**Result:** Nomu retail selling pressure **concentrates in months 2-4** post-IPO, creating price declines even for fundamentally sound companies.

## What Separates Winners from Losers?

Analyzing 2024-2025 cohorts reveals clear **success factors**.

### Aftermarket Winners (6-Month Returns >15%)

**Common Characteristics:**

**1. Beat Earnings Expectations:**
- First post-IPO quarter results exceeded prospectus guidance
- Revenue growth 20-30% (vs 15% guidance)
- EBITDA margins expanded (operational leverage demonstrated)

**Example: Flynas (+22%)**
- Q1 2025 earnings: SAR 185M EBITDA (vs SAR 165M guidance)
- Load factor: 88% (vs 84% guidance)
- Route expansion ahead of schedule
- **Market reaction:** Re-priced upward to reflect superior execution

---

**2. Sector Momentum Alignment:**
- Launched in sectors experiencing positive news flow
- Peer stocks outperforming (rising tide lifts all boats)
- Government policy support (Vision 2030 sectors)

**Example: Fakeeh Care (+18%)**
- Healthcare sector +8% during period (defensive strength)
- Medical tourism initiatives announced (government support)
- Competitor Sulaiman Al Habib trading at new highs
- **Market reaction:** Healthcare premium sustained

---

**3. Strategic Actions Post-IPO:**
- M&A announcements using IPO proceeds
- International expansion or partnerships
- New product launches or service lines
- ESG initiatives (attracting ESG-focused funds)

**Example: Saudi Parts Store (+15%)**
- Announced e-commerce platform 2 months post-IPO
- Partnership with regional logistics provider
- Store expansion plan revealed (20 new locations)
- **Market reaction:** Growth narrative reinforced

---

**4. Strong Liquidity Profile:**
- Daily volume exceeding SAR 15M (easy entry/exit)
- Institutional interest sustained (buying on dips)
- Analyst upgrades (price target increases)

---

### Aftermarket Losers (6-Month Returns <-15%)

**Common Characteristics:**

**1. Earnings Disappointments:**
- First post-IPO quarter missed prospectus projections
- Revenue growth slowed unexpectedly
- Margin compression (cost overruns, competitive pressure)

**Example: Alwazn Almithaly (-35%)**
- Q1 2025 earnings: SAR 12M EBITDA (vs SAR 18M guidance)
- Input costs spiked (raw materials)
- Customer attrition (competitive losses)
- **Market reaction:** Credibility destroyed, multiple compressed

---

**2. Overpricing at Launch:**
- Priced at peak multiples vs peers
- No first-day pop (or negative)
- Immediate underwater investors = selling pressure

**Example Pattern:**
```
Offer P/E: 18x (peers trading 14-15x)
First-day: -3% (overpricing signal)
Month 1-3: Continued decline as retail exits
Month 6: -25% (multiple compression to peer level)
```

---

**3. Sector Headwinds:**
- Launched into deteriorating sector conditions
- Peer stocks declining (sector rotation away)
- Regulatory changes or competitive threats

**Example: Medgulf Insurance (-8%)**
- Insurance sector underperformed (rate competition)
- Regulatory changes to pricing (margin pressure)
- Peer stocks down -12% average
- **Market reaction:** Sector sell-off dragged stock down

---

**4. Liquidity Collapse:**
- Daily volume dropped below SAR 2M (illiquid)
- Wide bid-ask spreads (2-4%) = exit difficult
- No institutional support (underwriter disappeared)
- Trapped retail investors (can't exit without loss)

## Underpricing vs Overpricing: The Optimal Balance

### The Underpricing Cost-Benefit Analysis

**Scenario: SAR 1 Billion IPO with 20% Underpricing**

**First-Day:**
- Offer price: SAR 50
- First-day close: SAR 60 (+20%)
- Market cap: SAR 4 billion (post-IPO)

**The "Money Left on Table":**
```
If priced at SAR 60 (instead of SAR 50):
- Capital raised: SAR 1.2B (vs SAR 1B actual)
- "Lost" proceeds: SAR 200M (20% underpricing)
```

**But Consider the Benefits:**
- **Aftermarket momentum:** Strong first day = sustained buying interest
- **Media coverage:** Positive headlines attract new investors
- **Employee morale:** Stock options immediately valuable
- **Future access:** Successful IPO = easier secondary offerings later

**Net Assessment:**
- Underpricing <10%: **Acceptable cost** for strong market entry
- Underpricing 10-15%: **Borderline** (significant value foregone)
- Underpricing >15%: **Excessive** (poor pricing execution)

---

### The Overpricing Disaster

**Scenario: SAR 500M IPO with -5% First-Day Return**

**First-Day:**
- Offer price: SAR 40
- First-day close: SAR 38 (-5%)
- Retail investors underwater immediately

**The Cascade Effect:**
```
Week 1: Retail panic selling (trying to minimize losses)
Month 1: Stock at SAR 35 (-12.5%)
Month 3: Stock at SAR 32 (-20%)
Month 6: Stock at SAR 30 (-25%)
```

**The Reputational Damage:**
- **Investor relations nightmare:** Constant complaints from retail
- **Institutional skepticism:** Future offerings face scrutiny
- **Management distraction:** Time spent managing stock price, not operations
- **Secondary offering impossible:** Can't raise more capital with stock down

**Net Assessment:**
- Overpricing (negative first-day) = **catastrophic mistake**
- Better to price 10-15% lower and ensure positive momentum
- **Rule:** Leave 5-10% upside for first-day investors

## When to Time Secondary Offerings

Secondary offerings (additional equity issuance post-IPO) require careful timing.

### Optimal Secondary Timing Windows

**Window 1: 12-18 Months Post-IPO (Most Common)**

**Prerequisites:**
- Stock trading **15-25% above offer price**
- 2-3 quarters of earnings beats (credibility established)
- Analyst coverage positive (3+ buy ratings)
- Market conditions favorable (index up 10%+ from IPO)

**Advantages:**
- Valuation 20-40% higher than IPO (raise more capital per share)
- Proven track record (reduces execution risk)
- Existing shareholder base (familiar story)

**Example: Hypothetical TASI Company**
```
IPO (Month 0): SAR 50/share, raised SAR 1B (30% float)
Month 12: Stock at SAR 62 (+24%)
Secondary offering: 10% additional float at SAR 60/share
Capital raised: SAR 640M (60% more per share than IPO)
```

---

**Window 2: 6-9 Months Post-IPO (Momentum Play)**

**Prerequisites:**
- Stock trading **30%+ above offer price** (exceptional performance)
- Major growth catalyst announced (M&A, expansion)
- Urgent capital need (time-sensitive opportunity)

**Advantages:**
- Capture extreme valuation (momentum pricing)
- Fund transformative growth immediately
- Dilution offset by growth prospects

**Risk:**
- If momentum fades, secondary fails (market rejects pricing)
- May be viewed as "cashing in" at peak (negative signal)

---

**Window 3: 24+ Months Post-IPO (Mature Issuer)**

**Prerequisites:**
- Stock in long-term uptrend (established track record)
- Multiple earnings cycles completed (maturity)
- Index inclusion achieved (passive demand)

**Advantages:**
- Issuer credibility maximized (proven execution)
- Analyst models refined (valuation confidence)
- Institutional relationships deep

---

### Red Flags: When NOT to Attempt Secondary

**❌ Stock Below IPO Offer Price:**
- Market won't support additional equity at loss
- Signals distress (destroys credibility)
- Wait for recovery before considering

**❌ Recent Earnings Miss:**
- Credibility damaged
- Institutional investors skeptical
- Wait 2-3 quarters of beats before attempting

**❌ Sector Downturn:**
- Peer stocks declining
- Sector rotation away (capital leaving)
- Wait for sector sentiment improvement

**❌ Market Correction (Index Down 10%+):**
- Risk-off environment
- Equity issuance difficult across board
- Delay until market stabilizes

## Strategic Recommendations: Maximizing Aftermarket Value

### For Companies Post-IPO

**Month 0-3 (Critical Period):**
- **Beat first earnings report** (most important single event)
- Maintain active IR (weekly updates to institutions)
- Execute announced plans (demonstrate credibility)
- Avoid negative surprises (pre-announce if needed)

**Month 3-6 (Building Momentum):**
- Announce strategic initiatives (M&A, expansion)
- Engage additional analysts (broaden coverage)
- Host investor day (deep dive on strategy)
- Consider ESG reporting (attract ESG funds)

**Month 6-12 (Positioning for Secondary):**
- Establish uptrend (consistent beats)
- Expand free float if possible (improve liquidity)
- Target index inclusion (passive inflows)
- Prepare secondary offering materials (ready when window opens)

---

### For Investors: When to Enter/Exit

**Entry Points:**
- **Day 1:** If first-day return <5% (underpricing minimal)
- **Month 1-2:** If earnings beat + stock consolidating (momentum incoming)
- **Month 3-6:** If trading below offer but fundamentals strong (value play)

**Exit Points:**
- **Day 1:** If first-day return >20% (excessive underpricing, take profit)
- **Post-earnings miss:** If company misses first quarter (credibility at risk)
- **Month 6:** If trading below offer with no recovery trajectory (cut losses)

## Key Takeaways: Aftermarket Success Factors

1. **First-Day Sweet Spot:** +5-10% optimal (TASI +8.5% average, Nomu +12.3% average); >20% = excessive underpricing, <0% = overpricing disaster

2. **6-Month Reality Check:** TASI +4.2% average (65% above offer), Nomu -15% average (42% above offer)—19-point performance gap reflects market structure, not just fundamentals

3. **Market Stress Test:** 2025's challenging environment (TASI -6.8%, Nomu -26%) revealed TASI IPOs' **resilience** (outperformed index by 11 points) vs Nomu vulnerability

4. **Winner Characteristics:** Beat earnings (+20-30% returns), sector momentum alignment, strategic post-IPO actions, strong liquidity (>SAR 15M daily volume)

5. **Loser Characteristics:** Earnings misses (-25%+ returns), overpricing at launch (negative first-day), sector headwinds, liquidity collapse (<SAR 2M daily)

6. **Underpricing Trade-Off:** <10% acceptable cost for momentum, 10-15% borderline, >15% excessive value foregone; but overpricing (negative first-day) = catastrophic

7. **TASI Quality Filter:** Higher listing standards (SAR 300M cap, 3yr financials, profitability) result in 65% above offer vs Nomu 42% (lower standards = lower success rate)

8. **Liquidity Amplifies Outcomes:** TASI's 15-30x higher volume provides downside protection during stress; Nomu's thin trading amplifies declines (forced selling, wide spreads)

9. **Secondary Timing:** Optimal window 12-18 months post-IPO when stock 15-25% above offer, 2-3 earnings beats completed, analyst coverage positive

10. **First Quarter Critical:** Post-IPO earnings beat is **single most important event** for aftermarket success—establishes credibility and momentum for next 12 months
